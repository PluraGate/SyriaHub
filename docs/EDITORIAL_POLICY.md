# Editorial Policy

> What belongs on SyriaHub, and why.

*Last Updated: March 2026*

---

## Scope

SyriaHub documents **Syria's built environment and social record** — the physical, spatial, and human consequences of conflict and reconstruction. Content is in scope if it contributes to understanding, documenting, or analysing:

- The physical state of Syria's built environment (buildings, infrastructure, urban fabric, heritage)
- Displacement, return, and reconstruction processes and their spatial dimensions
- Land, property, and tenure rights affected by conflict
- Environmental conditions relevant to reconstruction
- Governance, policy, and legal frameworks shaping reconstruction
- Oral history, testimony, and cultural memory connected to specific places or periods
- Methodologies for documenting or analysing any of the above

### Geographic scope
Primary focus: the territory of Syria. Secondary scope includes:
- Neighbouring countries as contexts for displacement and refugee dynamics (Lebanon, Jordan, Turkey, Iraq)
- Diaspora contexts where relevant to documentation of Syrian places, heritage, or records
- International policy and donor frameworks that directly affect Syrian reconstruction

### Temporal scope
No hard cutoff. Content may cover:
- Pre-conflict (before 2011) conditions that establish baselines for damage assessment
- The conflict period (2011–present) in all its phases
- Reconstruction and recovery processes ongoing from 2018 forward
- Historical and archival material predating 2011 where relevant to heritage or property records

---

## What Is NOT In Scope

| Out of scope | Reason |
|-------------|--------|
| General Middle East regional analysis unconnected to Syria | Too broad; dilutes the knowledge base |
| Political analysis of the Syrian conflict without built-environment or social-record angle | This is a documentation and research platform, not a political commentary platform |
| Opinions about what should happen in Syria | Prescriptive advocacy is out of scope; descriptive documentation is in scope |
| Content about Syria that does not contribute to the knowledge base (news reposts, social commentary) | Ephemeral content without research value |
| Personal attacks on individuals | Regardless of their role in the conflict |
| Content that could be used to identify, locate, or endanger individuals | Safety takes precedence over documentation completeness |

## Content Quality Standards

All content is expected to meet basic standards of honest documentation. The bar varies by content type.

### Articles
- Present a clear claim, analysis, or argument
- Cite primary sources or evidence
- Specify temporal and geographic scope
- Accurately declare evidence type and trust dimensions
- Use hedging language proportionate to the evidence quality: "appears to", "evidence suggests", "as of [date]"

### Traces (archival artefacts)
- Include artefact type and preservation status (original / copy / transcription)
- Provide as much provenance context as known — where, when, by whom
- Flag any personally identifying information that may create safety risk
- Do not describe as "original" if the item is a copy

### Research Gaps
- Clearly define what is missing and why it matters
- Specify scope (geographic, temporal, disciplinary)
- Distinguish between "no one has studied this" and "I haven't found it yet" — do a basic literature check before posting a gap

### Questions
- Be specific enough that an answer is possible
- Include context: what you already know, what you're trying to determine
- Tag with the relevant discipline

### Resources / Data
- Include provenance and data source
- Specify collection methodology if known
- Note known limitations, coverage gaps, or data quality issues

## Evidence Standards

SyriaHub uses a tiered evidence hierarchy. Authors must accurately classify their evidence.

| Tier | Types | What this means |
|------|-------|----------------|
| **Primary** | Field survey, ground photo, eyewitness testimony, laser scan, sensor data, satellite imagery | Direct observation or measurement |
| **Secondary** | Academic paper, NGO assessment, government document, news report | Processed primary evidence |
| **Derived** | BIM model, simulation, GIS layer, AI prediction | Built from primary or secondary sources |
| **Interpretive** | Policy brief, expert commentary, editorial analysis | Analysis and argument |

**Do not overstate your evidence tier.** A synthesis of NGO reports is secondary, not primary. Stating otherwise undermines the trust system.

## Sensitive Content

### Personally identifying information
Content that could identify specific individuals — their location, affiliations, movements, or activities — in a context where identification creates safety risk must be handled with care:

- Flag it before publishing so a moderator can review
- Redact names and identifying details unless the person is a public figure acting in a public role
- When in doubt, remove the identifying detail; the documentation remains useful without it

### Contested events
Syria's conflict involves deeply contested accounts of events. SyriaHub does not adjudicate which account is "true". Instead:
- Multiple accounts of the same event can coexist as separate posts, each with their own evidence and trust dimensions
- Cite contradictions explicitly using `disputes` citations
- Use hedging language: "according to [source]", "the following account is disputed"
- Do not present contested claims as established fact

### Sensitive locations
Certain locations are sensitive for security reasons (active military infrastructure, detention facilities, border crossings). Exercise judgment about the level of geographic specificity appropriate for such locations. If uncertain, use city/district-level spatial tagging rather than precise coordinates.

## Language

SyriaHub supports English and Arabic. Either is acceptable for all content types. Authors should use the language in which their evidence and sources are most accurately expressible.

If submitting in one language, translations of key terms or titles in the other language are encouraged but not required.

Transliterations of Arabic place names should use common practice; where multiple transliterations exist, include alternatives in the tags.

## Attribution and Licensing

All content requires author attribution. Pseudonymous submission is available for researchers facing safety constraints — contact moderators before registration to arrange.

Authors choose a license at publication:

| License | What it permits |
|---------|----------------|
| **CC BY 4.0** | Reuse with attribution (recommended for research data) |
| **CC BY-SA 4.0** | Reuse with attribution; derivatives must use same license |
| **CC BY-NC 4.0** | Non-commercial reuse with attribution |
| **All Rights Reserved** | Contact author before any reuse |

For Trace content (archival artefacts), the author is the person submitting the documentation, not necessarily the original creator of the artefact. If submitting material created by others, note this in the provenance and use a license consistent with your rights to the material.

---

## What Moderators Evaluate

Moderators assess whether content meets the standards above. They do **not** evaluate:

- Whether research conclusions are correct
- Whether a source's political position is acceptable
- Whether the content aligns with any particular narrative of the conflict

A post from an institutional source and a post from an affected resident are held to the same documentation standards. Neither is privileged by default.

---

## Relationship to the Trust System

Editorial policy sets the floor — what is acceptable to publish at all. The trust system (T1–T5) provides a richer signal about the epistemic quality of content that clears the floor. A post can meet editorial standards (citable, attributed, within scope) while still having low T2 (undocumented method) or low T5 (no corroboration). Both facts are represented.

The editorial policy is binary (in scope / not in scope). The trust system is continuous. They serve different purposes.
